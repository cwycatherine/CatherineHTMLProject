My finding Personality data set story

There has been a very popular test recently- 16 Personalities, that everyone can do to get a result of their personality type with a 5-letter-combination. Their website states that you can take “Only 10 minutes to get a “freakishly accurate” description of who you are and why you do things the way you do.”

The main component of the personality types are formed by specific traits, each letter acronyms represent a different trait:

  1. Extraverted (E) vs. Introverted (I)
  2. Sensing (S) vs. Intuitive (N)
  3. Thinking (T) vs. Feeling (F)
  4. Judging (J) vs. Perceiving (P)
  5. Assertive (A) vs. Turbulent (T)

They aim to “fit the complexities of human personality into well-defined models and make people more aware of their personality.” They declare that they only outline indicators and tendencies, but not definitive answers or guidelines on how individuals actually behave.

Personality Data Exploration

1. Data source:

Yes, I got my data from GitHub, but where exactly are those data coming from?

From reading the README file, I learned that the data consist of Big Five personality trait scores for 307,313 individuals from various countries. These scores were calculated by Github user gc following the official guidelines provided by the International Personality Item Pool (IPIP) using raw data from the IPIP-NEO-300 questionnaire, a 300-item assessment recognized as the most accurate public domain personality test available.

We can see that there are 9 variables, and 307,313 observations. I believe that the data was collected in 2015 since that’s the date shown on Github.

The agreeable score includes attributes such as trust, altruism, kindness. For example, the higher the score is, the more the individual tend to be liked by their peers and the more likely they will serve in community leadership roles, engage in volunteer works, and such.

The extraversion score represents the excitability, sociability, talkativeness and high amounts of emotional expressiveness.A higher score would mean mean that the person is more extraverted and willing to connect with people.

Openness refers to an individual’s openness to experience. People who score high on this tend to have more curiosity and are more willing to explore new things.

Conscientiousness is measured by thoughtfulness, good impulse control, and goal-directed behaviors. Scoring high in it would reflect a good planning habit, and are mindful of structure and time.

Lastly, the neuroticism score shows one’s emotional instability, such as sadness, moodiness. People who are high in neuroticism tend to have more anxiety, experience more mood swings and less resilient emotionally.

Each score is calculated from out of 1, therefore, we can see many scores in the format of e.g. 0.65, 0.78… I have taken these explanations and definitions from verywellmind.com.

cor.mtest <- function(mat, conf.level = 0.95) {
  mat <- as.matrix(mat)
  n <- ncol(mat)
  p.mat <- matrix(NA, n, n)
  diag(p.mat) <- 0
  for (i in 1:(n - 1)) {
    for (j in (i + 1):n) {
      test <- cor.test(mat[, i], mat[, j], conf.level = conf.level)
      p.mat[i, j] <- p.mat[j, i] <- test$p.value
    }
  }
  colnames(p.mat) <- rownames(p.mat) <- colnames(mat)
  return(list(p = p.mat))
}

This chunk defines a helper function, cor.mtest, to calculate p-values for each pair of variables in the correlation matrix. These p-values indicate whether the correlations are statistically significant. The function outputs a matrix of p-values for use in the visualization later.
